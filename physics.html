Physics

Discover the fundamental laws that govern the universe

Chapter 1: Foundations of Physics

Physics is the branch of science that studies matter, energy, motion, forces, and how everything in the universe behaves.

1.1 What is Physics?

Physics explains fundamental phenomena in our world:

  • Why a ball falls down → Gravity
  • Why a car moves → Force & motion
  • Why we see light → Optics

1.2 Physical Quantities

A physical quantity is anything that can be measured.

Fundamental Quantities:

  • Length (meter, m)
  • Mass (kilogram, kg)
  • Time (second, s)
  • Temperature (kelvin, K)
  • Electric current (ampere, A)
  • Amount of substance (mole, mol)
  • Luminous intensity (candela, cd)

Derived Quantities:

Velocity = distance ÷ time
Force = mass × acceleration
Density = mass ÷ volume

1.3 SI Units

Standard system used worldwide:

  • Length → meter (m)
  • Mass → kilogram (kg)
  • Time → second (s)

1.4 Measurement Instruments

  • Meter rule → length
  • Vernier caliper → more accurate length
  • Micrometer screw gauge → very small measurements
  • Stopwatch → time

1.5 Scalars and Vectors

Scalars: Magnitude only (mass, time, temperature)
Vectors: Magnitude + direction (velocity, force, displacement)

Example: 5 m → scalar | 5 m North → vector

1.6 Measurement Errors

  • Systematic error: Faulty instrument
  • Random error: Human mistakes

1.7 Accuracy vs Precision

  • Accuracy: Close to true value
  • Precision: Consistency

1.8 Dimensional Analysis

Used to check equations and convert units:

Velocity → m/s
Force → kg·m/s²

Chapter 2: Mechanics (Motion & Forces)

Mechanics studies motion, forces, energy, and how objects move and interact. Kinematics is the study of motion without considering the cause (force).

2.1 Key Terms

Distance: Total path traveled (scalar)
Displacement: Shortest path in a direction (vector)

Example: Walking 10 m in a circle → distance = 10 m, displacement = 0 m

2.2 Speed and Velocity

Speed = distance ÷ time (scalar)
Velocity = displacement ÷ time (vector)

Example: 100 m in 10 s → Speed = 10 m/s
With direction → Velocity = 10 m/s North

2.3 Acceleration

Rate of change of velocity:

a = (v - u) / t
Where: a = acceleration, v = final velocity, u = initial velocity, t = time

Example: 0 m/s to 20 m/s in 5 s → a = 4 m/s²

2.4 Equations of Motion

v = u + at
s = ut + ½at²
v² = u² + 2as

Example: u = 0, a = 2 m/s², t = 3 s → v = 6 m/s

2.5 Newton's Laws of Motion

First Law: Object remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by a force

Second Law: F = ma
Example: m = 5 kg, a = 2 m/s² → F = 10 N

Third Law: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction

2.6 Friction

Force that opposes motion:

  • Static friction
  • Kinetic friction

2.7 Circular Motion

Motion in a circle requires centripetal force and involves continuous change in direction.

2.8 Work, Energy, and Power

Work: W = F × d
Kinetic Energy: KE = ½mv²
Potential Energy: PE = mgh
Power: P = W / t

Example: 100 J in 5 s → Power = 20 W

2.9 Momentum and Impulse

Momentum: p = mv
Impulse: Ft = Δp

Law of Conservation of Momentum:
Total momentum before = Total momentum after

2.10 Gravitation

Force of attraction between masses:

F = G(m₁m₂/r²)

Gravity causes objects to fall toward Earth

Chapter 3: Properties of Matter

Properties of matter describe how substances behave when forces are applied, heated, cooled, or when they flow and deform.

3.1 Elasticity

Ability of a material to return to its original shape and size after removing the applied force.

Types of Deformation:

  • Elastic deformation: Temporary (returns back)
  • Plastic deformation: Permanent (does NOT return)

Hooke's Law:

F = kx
Where: F = applied force, k = spring constant, x = extension

Applies only within the elastic limit

Stress and Strain:

Stress = F / A (Force per unit area)
Strain = ΔL / L (Ratio of extension to original length)

Young's Modulus = Stress / Strain
(Measures how stiff a material is)

3.2 Surface Tension

Force that makes the surface of a liquid act like a tight stretched membrane.

  • Cause: Molecules at surface pulled inward
  • Effects: Liquids form spherical drops, surface resists breaking
  • Examples: Water droplets are round, needle floats on water, insects walk on water

3.3 Viscosity

Internal resistance of a fluid to flow.

  • High viscosity: Flows slowly (honey, oil)
  • Low viscosity: Flows easily (water, alcohol)

Factors Affecting Viscosity:

  • Liquids → viscosity decreases when heated
  • Gases → viscosity increases when heated

3.4 Pressure in Fluids

P = F / A

Pressure in Liquid: P = ρgh
Where: ρ = density, g = gravity, h = depth

Key Principles:

  • Pressure increases with depth
  • Pressure acts in all directions
  • Same depth → same pressure

3.5 Density

Density = mass / volume

High density → tightly packed particles
Low density → loosely packed particles

Examples: Iron sinks (high density), Wood floats (lower density than water)

3.6 Flotation (Buoyancy)

Ability of an object to float in a fluid.

Archimedes' Principle:

Upthrust = weight of fluid displaced

Object floats → Upthrust ≥ Weight
Object sinks → Weight > Upthrust

Examples:

  • Ship: Large volume displaces more water, upthrust balances weight → floats
  • Stone: Small volume, heavy → sinks
  • Ice: Less dense than water → floats

Chapter 4: Heat & Thermodynamics

Heat is a form of energy that flows from a hotter body to a colder body. Thermodynamics is the study of heat, temperature, and energy transfer.

4.1 Temperature & Thermometers

Temperature is the measure of how hot or cold a body is (average kinetic energy of particles).

Temperature Scales:

  • Celsius (°C)
  • Kelvin (K) → SI unit
  • Fahrenheit (°F)
Conversion: K = °C + 273

Types of Thermometers:

  • Liquid-in-glass (mercury/alcohol)
  • Digital thermometer
  • Thermocouple

4.2 Heat Transfer

(A) Conduction:

Heat transfer through a material without movement of the material.

  • Particles vibrate and pass energy to neighbors
  • Examples: Metal spoon gets hot in tea, cooking pot heats up

(B) Convection:

Heat transfer through movement of fluids.

  • Hot fluid rises, cold fluid sinks
  • Examples: Boiling water circulation, sea breeze & land breeze

(C) Radiation:

Heat transfer through electromagnetic waves (does NOT require a medium).

  • Examples: Heat from the Sun, feeling heat from fire

4.3 Gas Laws

Boyle's Law (Constant Temperature):

P ∝ 1/V
Pressure increases when volume decreases

Charles's Law (Constant Pressure):

V ∝ T
Volume increases with temperature

Pressure Law (Constant Volume):

P ∝ T
Pressure increases with temperature

Combined Gas Law:

PV / T = constant

4.4 First Law of Thermodynamics

Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed.

Q = ΔU + W
Where: Q = heat supplied, ΔU = change in internal energy, W = work done

4.5 Second Law of Thermodynamics

Heat cannot flow from a colder body to a hotter body without external work.

  • Heat flows naturally: Hot → Cold
  • No machine is 100% efficient
  • Some energy is always lost as heat

4.6 Heat Capacity & Specific Heat

Q = mcΔT
Where: m = mass, c = specific heat, ΔT = temperature change

Example: Water heats slowly → high specific heat

4.7 Change of State

Matter changes state when heat is added or removed:

  • Melting (solid → liquid)
  • Freezing (liquid → solid)
  • Boiling (liquid → gas)
  • Condensation (gas → liquid)

Latent Heat:

Heat required to change state without temperature change.

Example: Ice melts at 0°C but temperature stays constant.

Chapter 5: Waves & Oscillations

A wave is a disturbance that transfers energy from one place to another without transporting matter permanently.

5.1 Types of Waves

Mechanical Waves:

  • Require a medium to travel
  • Cannot travel in vacuum
  • Examples: Sound waves, water waves

Electromagnetic Waves:

  • Do NOT require a medium
  • Can travel in vacuum
  • Examples: Light, radio waves, X-rays

Transverse Waves:

  • Particles move perpendicular to wave direction
  • Examples: Light waves, water waves

Longitudinal Waves:

  • Particles move parallel to wave direction
  • Example: Sound waves

5.2 Wave Properties

Amplitude (A): Maximum displacement from equilibrium
Wavelength (λ): Distance between two consecutive identical points (meters)
Frequency (f): Number of oscillations per second (Hz)
Period (T): Time for one complete oscillation

T = 1 / f

Wave Speed: v = fλ

5.3 Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

Motion where the restoring force is proportional to displacement and direction is always toward equilibrium.

F = -kx
Where: F = restoring force, k = spring constant, x = displacement

Time Period for a spring: T = 2π √(m/k)
Where: m = mass, k = spring constant

Key Idea: More mass → slower oscillation | Stiffer spring → faster oscillation

5.4 Energy in SHM

  • Total energy is constant
  • Energy transforms between Kinetic Energy (KE) and Potential Energy (PE)
  • At mean position → KE is maximum
  • At extreme positions → PE is maximum

5.5 Sound Waves

  • Sound is a longitudinal wave
  • Requires a medium (cannot travel in vacuum)
  • Faster in solids, slower in gases

Characteristics of Sound:

  • Pitch: Depends on frequency (high frequency → high pitch)
  • Loudness: Depends on amplitude (higher amplitude → louder)
  • Quality (Timbre): Distinguishes different sounds

5.6 Doppler Effect

Change in frequency due to relative motion between source and observer.

  • Approaching → frequency increases
  • Receding → frequency decreases
  • Example: Ambulance siren sounds higher when approaching, lower when moving away

5.7 Superposition of Waves

When two waves meet, the resultant displacement is the sum of individual displacements.

Types:

  • Constructive Interference: Waves add up, amplitude increases
  • Destructive Interference: Waves cancel out, amplitude decreases

5.8 Standing Waves

Formed when two waves of same frequency move in opposite directions.

  • Nodes: Zero displacement
  • Antinodes: Maximum displacement
  • Examples: Vibrating guitar string, air columns in pipes

Chapter 6: Optics (Light)

Optics is the study of light and its behavior such as reflection, refraction, and image formation.

6.1 Nature of Light

  • Light is an electromagnetic wave
  • Does NOT need a medium
  • Can travel through vacuum
  • Speed in vacuum: ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s

Dual Nature of Light:

Light behaves as both wave and particle (photons).

6.2 Reflection of Light

Bouncing back of light when it hits a surface.

Laws of Reflection:

  1. Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection
  2. Incident ray, reflected ray, and normal lie in the same plane

Types of Reflection:

  • Regular Reflection: Smooth surface, clear image (mirror)
  • Diffuse Reflection: Rough surface, scattered light (wall)

6.3 Mirrors

Plane Mirror:

  • Flat surface
  • Forms virtual, upright image
  • Same size, laterally inverted

Concave Mirror:

  • Curved inward
  • Can form real or virtual images
  • Magnified or diminished images

Convex Mirror:

  • Curved outward
  • Always forms virtual, upright, smaller image
Mirror Formula: 1/f = 1/u + 1/v
Where: f = focal length, u = object distance, v = image distance

Magnification: m = v/u

6.4 Refraction of Light

Bending of light when it passes from one medium to another (due to change in speed).

Snell's Law:

n₁ sinθ₁ = n₂ sinθ₂

Refractive Index: n = c/v
Where: c = speed in vacuum, v = speed in medium

Effects of Refraction:

  • Object appears bent
  • Objects in water look closer
  • Mirage effect

6.5 Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

Conditions:

  • Light moves from denser → rarer medium
  • Angle of incidence > critical angle

Applications: Optical fibers, endoscopes

6.6 Lenses

Convex Lens (Converging):

  • Converges light rays
  • Can form real or virtual images

Concave Lens (Diverging):

  • Diverges light rays
  • Always forms virtual images
Lens Formula: 1/f = 1/v - 1/u
Magnification: m = v/u

6.7 Optical Instruments

  • Microscope: Views very small objects using convex lenses
  • Telescope: Views distant objects, magnifies far objects

6.8 Dispersion of Light

Splitting of white light into its component colors.

Spectrum Order: Red → Orange → Yellow → Green → Blue → Indigo → Violet

Example: Prism splitting sunlight

6.9 Human Eye & Vision

Parts: Cornea, Iris, Pupil, Lens, Retina

Real, inverted image formed on retina; brain interprets as upright.

Common Defects:

  • Myopia (Short-sightedness): Near objects clear, distant blurred
  • Hypermetropia (Long-sightedness): Far objects clear, near blurred

Chapter 7: Electricity

Electricity is the flow of electric charge through a conductor.

7.1 Electrostatics

Study of charges at rest.

Types of Charges:

  • Positive (+) charge
  • Negative (−) charge
Law of Charges:
Like charges repel | Unlike charges attract

Coulomb's Law:

F = k (q₁q₂ / r²)
Where: F = force, q = charges, r = distance

Key Idea: Force increases with charge, decreases with distance

7.2 Electric Field

Region around a charge where another charge experiences force.

Electric Field Strength: E = F/q

Key Points:
- Field lines go from + to −
- Stronger field → lines closer together

7.3 Electric Potential

Work done to move a unit charge.

V = W/q
Where: V = potential, W = work done, q = charge

Key Idea: High potential → more energy | Current flows from high → low potential

7.4 Capacitance

Ability of a system to store electric charge.

C = Q/V
Where: C = capacitance (Farad, F), Q = charge, V = voltage

7.5 Current Electricity

Electric Current: I = Q/t
Where: I = current (Ampere, A), Q = charge, t = time

Direction: Conventional current flows from + to − | Electron flow is opposite

7.6 Ohm's Law

Current through a conductor is directly proportional to voltage (at constant temperature).

V = IR
Where: V = voltage, I = current, R = resistance

Key Idea: More voltage → more current | More resistance → less current

7.7 Resistance

Opposition to flow of current.

R = V/I

Factors Affecting Resistance:
- Length ↑ → Resistance ↑
- Area ↑ → Resistance ↓
- Temperature ↑ → Resistance ↑

7.8 Electrical Circuits

Components: Battery (source), Resistor, Switch, Wires

Series Circuit:

  • Components connected in a line
  • Same current flows through all
R = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...

Parallel Circuit:

  • Multiple paths for current
  • Same voltage across components
1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...

7.9 Electrical Power & Energy

Power: P = VI = I²R = V²/R
Unit: Watt (W)

Electrical Energy: E = Pt

7.10 Heating Effect of Current

Joule's Law: H = I²Rt

Applications: Electric heater, Iron, Fuse

7.11 Safety Devices

  • Fuse: Protects circuit from excess current, melts when current is too high
  • Circuit Breaker: Automatically switches off circuit during faults

Chapter 8: Magnetism & Electromagnetism

Magnetism is the property of certain materials to attract iron or produce a magnetic field.

8.1 Key Concepts

  • Magnetic materials: iron, cobalt, nickel
  • Magnets have north (N) and south (S) poles
  • Like poles repel, unlike poles attract

8.2 Magnetic Field

Region around a magnet where a force acts on another magnet or moving charge.

Magnetic Field Lines:

  • Lines go from N → S outside magnet
  • Lines inside magnet go from S → N
  • Closer lines → stronger field

8.3 Electromagnets

Magnet produced by electric current flowing through a wire.

  • More current → stronger magnetic field
  • More coils → stronger electromagnet

Applications: Electric bell, lifting heavy iron objects, motors & generators

8.4 Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

Fleming's Left-Hand Rule:

  • Thumb → Motion
  • First finger → Field
  • Second finger → Current
Magnetic Force: F = BIL sinθ
Where: B = magnetic field strength, I = current, L = length, θ = angle

8.5 Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday's Law:

Induced EMF in a coil is proportional to rate of change of magnetic flux.

ε = - dΦ/dt
Where: ε = induced EMF, Φ = magnetic flux

Lenz's Law:

Induced current opposes the change producing it.

Applications: Electric generators, transformers, induction cookers

8.6 Alternating Current (AC)

  • Current that reverses direction periodically
  • Household supply is AC
  • Frequency: 50–60 Hz (most countries)

Advantages: Can be transmitted long distances efficiently, voltage can be stepped up or down

8.7 Direct Current (DC)

  • Current that flows in one direction only
  • Examples: Batteries, solar cells

8.8 Transformers

Change AC voltage:

  • Step-up → increase voltage
  • Step-down → decrease voltage
Vₛ / Vₚ = Nₛ / Nₚ
Where: V = voltage, N = number of coils
s = secondary, p = primary

8.9 Electromagnetic Waves

  • Produced by oscillating electric and magnetic fields
  • Travel at speed of light
  • Examples: Radio waves, microwaves, X-rays

Chapter 9: Modern Physics

Modern Physics deals with phenomena that cannot be explained by classical physics, especially very small scales (atoms, nuclei) and very high speeds (near speed of light).

9.1 Atomic Structure

An atom is made of:

  • Protons (+)
  • Neutrons (0)
  • Electrons (−)

Atomic Models:

  • Rutherford Model: Dense nucleus, electrons orbit around
  • Bohr Model: Electrons move in fixed energy levels (shells), energy is quantized

Key Idea: Electrons cannot exist between energy levels; they move by absorbing or emitting energy.

9.2 Quantization of Energy

Energy exists in discrete packets (quanta), not continuous.

Photon Energy: E = hf
Where: E = energy, h = Planck's constant, f = frequency

Key Idea: Higher frequency → higher energy

9.3 Photoelectric Effect

Emission of electrons from a metal surface when light shines on it.

  • Only light above a certain frequency can emit electrons
  • Increasing intensity increases number of electrons, not energy

Einstein's Explanation: Light consists of photons (energy packets).

9.4 Wave-Particle Duality

Particles like electrons and light behave as both waves and particles.

  • Light shows interference (wave)
  • Also behaves as photons (particles)

9.5 De Broglie Wavelength

λ = h / p
Where: λ = wavelength, h = Planck's constant, p = momentum

Meaning: Every moving particle has a wavelength

9.6 Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle

It is impossible to know both exact position and exact momentum at the same time.

The more accurately you know one, the less you know the other.

9.7 Nuclear Physics

Study of the nucleus of an atom.

  • Nuclear forces are very strong and act over very short distances

Types of Nuclear Reactions:

  • Nuclear Fission: Splitting of a heavy nucleus, releases large energy
  • Nuclear Fusion: Combining light nuclei, powers the Sun

9.8 Radioactivity

Spontaneous emission of radiation from unstable nuclei.

Types of Radiation:

  • Alpha (α): Helium nucleus, low penetration
  • Beta (β): Electron, medium penetration
  • Gamma (γ): Electromagnetic wave, very high penetration

Key Idea: Radioactive decay reduces instability.

9.9 Half-Life

Time required for half of a radioactive substance to decay.

Each radioactive material has a fixed half-life.

9.10 Einstein's Relativity (Basic)

  • Time and space are not absolute
  • They depend on motion

Key Concepts:

  • Time dilation (time slows at high speeds)
  • Mass-energy equivalence
Famous Equation: E = mc²
